## The Question That Puzzled Physics for a Century

For the past 100 years, physicists have measured two mysterious numbers that appear in the fundamental laws of nature:

**Number 1: ~137**
```
The fine structure constant: α ≈ 1/137.036
```
This number determines the strength of electromagnetic interactions—how strongly light interacts with matter, how tightly electrons bind to atoms, and ultimately why chemistry works the way it does. Richard Feynman called it "one of the greatest damn mysteries of physics: a magic number that comes to us with no understanding by man."

**Number 2: ~0.23**
```
The Weinberg angle: sin²θ_W ≈ 0.23122
```
This number determines how two fundamental forces—electromagnetism and the weak nuclear force—"mix" together in the unified electroweak theory. It's like asking: when you blend red and blue paint, why is it 23% red and 77% blue, rather than 50-50 or any other proportion?

**The problem:** No one could explain *why* these specific values. They had to be measured experimentally and accepted as given facts—"that's just how the universe is."

Until now.
