## Abstract
We report an empirical correlation between a structural index `k` derived from dimensional analysis and the number of independently measurable properties in fundamental leptons. For the electron (e⁻), muon (μ⁻), and tau (τ⁻), the index `k`—defined as the sum of absolute values of dimensional exponents in a `T^(k₁) × T^(k₂)` decomposition—exactly matches the count of observable quantum properties. Specifically: electron (k=5, 5 observables), muon (k=6, 6 observables), tau (k=10, 10 observables). We extend this framework tentatively to other Standard Model particles and propose that structural index `k` may serve as a predictor of particle complexity. The pattern suggests that negative exponents (T^(-n)) correspond to dynamical or unstable properties, while positive exponents correspond to static attributes. This work presents the correlation as an empirical organizing principle requiring theoretical explanation.

**Keywords:** dimensional analysis, particle structure, lepton properties, observables, structural index

## I. Introduction
### 1.1 Motivation
The Standard Model of particle physics successfully describes fundamental particles and their interactions through gauge symmetries and quantum field theory. However, the origin of certain patterns—such as the existence of exactly three lepton generations, the hierarchy of masses spanning six orders of magnitude, and the specific set of quantum numbers characterizing each particle—remains unexplained from first principles.

Dimensional analysis has proven invaluable in physics, from Buckingham's Π theorem to renormalization group theory. Recently, systematic studies of physical divergences have revealed correlations between dimensional structure and observable behavior [1]. Here we investigate whether a simple structural index derived from dimensional decomposition correlates with the number of independently measurable properties of fundamental particles.

### 1.2 Defining the Structural Index
We define a structural index `k` for a particle characterized by dimensional structure `T^(k₁) × T^(k₂)`, where:
- `T` represents a fundamental unit (time, length, or mass dimension)
- `k₁, k₂` are integer exponents (positive, negative, or zero)

The structural index is defined as: `k = |k₁| + |k₂|`

The absolute value ensures that both "closed" (k > 0) and "open" (k < 0) boundary conditions contribute to system complexity.

**Key interpretation:**
- Each unit of `|k|` corresponds to one independently measurable property
- Negative exponents (`T^(-n)`) indicate dynamical or unstable properties
- Positive exponents (`T^(n)`) indicate static or geometric properties
- `T^(0)` represents dimensionless or pure existence (no measurable property)

### 1.3 Scope and Approach
This paper:
1. Documents the perfect correlation (k ↔ observables) for the three charged leptons
2. Tentatively extends the framework to neutrinos, quarks, and gauge bosons
3. Proposes testable predictions regarding particle stability
4. Discusses limitations and alternative interpretations

We emphasize that this work presents an empirical pattern rather than a derived theory. The correlation may point to deeper structural principles that remain to be formalized.

## II. Structural Decomposition of Leptons
### 2.1 Electron (e⁻)
**Proposed structure:** `T^(0) × T^(-5)`
**Decomposition:**
- `T^(0)`: Dimensionless component (k₁ = 0) → pure existence marker
- `T^(-5)`: Inverse volume dimension (k₂ = -5) → volumetric excitation

**Structural index:** `k = |0| + |-5| = 5`
**Independently measurable properties:**
1. Rest mass (m_e = 0.511 MeV/c²)
2. Electric charge (e = -1.602 × 10^(-19) C)
3. Spin projection (s_z = ±ħ/2)
4. Magnetic moment (μ_e = -9.285 × 10^(-24) J/T)
5. Lepton number (L_e = 1)

**Additional quantum numbers** (derived, not independent): Helicity, Weak isospin (T₃ = -1/2), Hypercharge (Y = -1)
**Result:** 5 independent observables ✓

**Physical interpretation:** The electron is stable (infinite lifetime) and lacks temporal dynamism, consistent with absence of `T^(-1)` component. The `T^(-5)` structure manifests as the extended quantum wavefunction.

### 2.2 Muon (μ⁻)
**Proposed structure:** `T^(-1) × T^(-5)`
**Decomposition:**
- `T^(-1)`: Inverse time dimension (k₁ = -1) → temporal variation
- `T^(-5)`: Inverse volume dimension (k₂ = -5) → volumetric excitation

**Structural index:** `k = |-1| + |-5| = 6`
**Independently measurable properties:**
1. Rest mass (m_μ = 105.66 MeV/c²)
2. Electric charge (e)
3. Spin projection (s_z)
4. Magnetic moment (μ_μ)
5. Lepton number (L_μ = 1)
6. Lifetime (τ_μ = 2.197 μs) ← New property from T^(-1)

**Result:** 6 independent observables ✓

**Physical interpretation:** The additional `T^(-1)` component introduces temporal instability. The muon decays via: `μ⁻ → e⁻ + ν̄_e + ν_μ`. The finite lifetime τ_μ directly corresponds to `T^(-1)`.

**Implication:** The factor 12π in muon's anomalous magnetic moment may originate from `T^(-1) × T^(-5)` structure.

### 2.3 Tau (τ⁻)
**Proposed structure:** `T^(-2) × T^(-8)`
**Decomposition:**
- `T^(-2)`: Inverse area dimension (k₁ = -2) → spatial variation
- `T^(-8)`: Higher-order inverse dimension (k₂ = -8) → configurational complexity

**Structural index:** `k = |-2| + |-8| = 10`
**Independently measurable properties:**
1. Rest mass (m_τ = 1776.86 MeV/c²)
2. Electric charge (e)
3. Spin projection (s_z)
4. Magnetic moment (μ_τ)
5. Lepton number (L_τ = 1)
6. Lifetime (τ_τ = 290.3 fs)
7-10. Four dominant decay modes with measurable branching ratios

**Result:** 10 independent observables ✓

**Physical interpretation:** `T^(-2)` introduces spatial complexity. `T^(-8)` suggests eight configurational degrees of freedom.

**Prediction:** Tau's anomalous magnetic moment should exceed SM predictions by ~7.6%.
